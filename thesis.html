PhD Thesis: Exact and approximate modeling in the behavioral setting

Ivan Markovsky

Promotors: Sabine Van Huffel and Bart De Moor

Abstract

We consider exact and approximate fitting of data by linear, bilinear, and quadratic static models and linear dynamic models. The problems are posed in the behavioral setting, i.e., the models are viewed as sets of possible outcomes and are not a priori bound to particular representations. Such a formulation gives freedom in the choice of the most suitable representation for a particular purpose.

In the linear static case, we consider weighted and structured total least squares problems. In general, both problems do have a closed form solution. We use local optimization methods and present alternative algorithms resulting from different parameterizations and optimization methods. The main emphasis is on the numerical efficiency of the algorithms, achieved by exploiting the special structure of the involved matrices. The bilinear and quadratic static approximation problems occur in computer vision applications: fundamental matrix and ellipsoid estimation. We solve these problems by the adjusted least squares method, which is a non-iterative method.

Data fitting by dynamic models is the subject of system identification. First we treat the exact identification problem: given a finite time series find the least complex linear time-invariant system that explains the data. We present algorithms aiming at difference equation and input/state/output representation of the system. The latter are closely related to the subspace identification methods. The considered approximate identification problem is: minimize the distance from a given time series to a trajectory of the approximating system subject to the constraint that the approximating system is of a bounded complexity.
