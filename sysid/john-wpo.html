Goal

The goal of this lab is for you to gain experience with the tools for identifying LTI systems described during the lectures. This will be applied to measurements on a real dynamic system. Either, you can use one of the systems available at the department, or you can suggest another system, e.g. which would be of interest for your master thesis.

Typical project

The lecture notes cover a fairly wide range of topics, impossible to be all covered during a single lab. In addition, different students have different interests. Therefore, the tasks enumerated below are just an example of what you can do during this lab:

  1. Design an excitation signal, for instance a multisine with holes, to trap nonlinear distortions.

  2. Apply the signal to the system.

  3. Distinguish the nonlinear distortions from the noise via the use of the robust and/or the fast method

  4. Obtain a non-parametric estimate of the BLA of the system, with a quantification of its uncertainty

  5. Use the Frequency Domain Identification toolbox (see below to make a parametric model of the BLA (or implement the identification algorithm yourself)

  6. Apply model order selection criteria (AIC, whiteness test of the residuals, value of the cost function,…)

Additional points of interest are

  • make experiments at different setpoints, and compare the BLA’s (important if you want to classify the nonlinear systems, see the course on nonlinear systems),

  • perform an experiment where the setpoint is varying during the experiment, and compute the BLTIA (Best Linear Time Invariant Approximation) of the system.

Toolbox

The frequency domain identification toolbox can be downloaded from http://booksupport.wiley.com/ (search for Author: Pintelon).
